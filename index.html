Contemporary built-environments are constructed as much by digital information as they are by

plasterboard and timber stud walls. The Digital Fun Palace investigates the co-dependent relationships

between digital interfaces (websites, apps, information etc.) and physical interfaces (walls, floors,

roofs etc.) in the design of a prototype for contemporary activity on a remote site in rural Victoria.

.


  • ...............................

The prototype for contemporary activities accommodates activities of a diverse and changing group

of between 1-25 visitors. The physical architecture of the prototype, paired with its digital systems

will enable multiple visitors to undertake activities that they are not able to undertake in their

current environment. Diverse activity and ownership of the prototype will be enabled by integrated

and roaming digital interfaces developed by the studio. The remote nature of the site is understood

as a condition of otherness, facilitating potential escape from conventions in behaviour. The digital

interface will also support unprescribed relationships between the visitors.

The design approach focuses on two design processes, whereby the material resolution of physical

interfaces is tested through physical modelling and digital interfaces through interactive layouts.

Developing a co-dependant physical and digital interface influenced by current digital technologies

constructing environments (Nest, Amazon Dash, Screens etc.) and focus on the current day application

of extensions of these beyond sustainability, comfort and security.
